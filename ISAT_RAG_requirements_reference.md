# ISAT Requirements Reference for RAG

## Purpose

This document is written to help a retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) system answer questions about how the ISAT major requirements work at James Madison University.

This file should be treated as a planning and explanation reference, not as a substitute for official advising. When there is uncertainty, course availability changes, or a tailored concentration question, the system should tell the user to confirm with an advisor.

## Core Degree Structure

The ISAT program totals **120 credit hours**.

The curriculum is built from:

- lower-level core requirements
- junior and senior holistic problem-solving courses
- strategic sector coursework
- one concentration area, unless the student is doing a tailored plan or approved variation

## Lower-Level Core Requirements

This is a four-course sequence focused on science and technology in the context of major social issues.

Required courses:

- **ISAT 112. Environmental Issues in Science and Technology** — 4 credits
- **ISAT 113. Biotechnology Issues in Science and Technology** — 3 credits
- **ISAT 113L. Biotechnology Issues in Science and Technology Laboratory** — 1 credit
- **ISAT 211. Issues in Modern Production Systems** — 3 credits
- **ISAT 212. Energy Issues in Science and Technology** — 3 credits

### 2) Analytical Methods: 17 credit hours

This sequence provides analytical tools used across ISAT.

Required courses:

- **ISAT 152. Topics in Applied Physics in ISAT** — 4 credits
- **ISAT 251. Topics in Applied Statistics in ISAT** — 3 credits
- **ISAT 252. Programming and Problem Solving** — 3 credits
- **ISAT 300. Applied Computing, Instrumentation and Measurement** — 3 credits

### 3) Social Context of Technology and Science: 6 credit hours

This sequence covers social, ethical, economic, and legal issues related to science and technology.

Required courses:

- **ISAT 171. Governance of Sociotechnical Systems** — 3 credits
- **ISAT 271. Technology, Science and Society** — 3 credits

## Junior and Senior Courses

### 4) Holistic Problem Solving: 10 credit hours

This sequence develops systems thinking and complex problem-solving skills.

Required courses:

- **ISAT 190. Introduction to Holistic Problem Solving in the ISAT Major** — 1 credit
- **ISAT 290. Introduction to Systems Thinking for Complex Problems** — 3 credits
- **ISAT 390. Holistic Problem-Solving Workshop I** — 3 credits
- **ISAT 391. Holistic Problem-Solving Workshop II** — 3 credits

### 5) Strategic Sectors in Science and Technology: 13–14 credit hours

Students complete strategic sector instruction during the junior year.

The strategic sectors are:

- Applied Biotechnology
- Applied Computing
- Energy
- Environment and Sustainability
- Industrial and Manufacturing Systems

Important interpretation rule:

- Students must choose **two sectors**.
- They complete the **full sector sequence for each of those two sectors**.
- Sector-level classes are generally **300-level**.
- Concentration classes are generally **400-level**.

## How Sectors and Concentrations Work

This is one of the most important rules for the RAG system to understand.

### Main rule

To complete a concentration, a student must:

1. complete the required **sector courses** for that concentration area, and
2. complete the required **400-level concentration courses/electives** for that same area, and
3. complete **one additional sector** in a second area, but **without** also doing that second area's concentration electives unless they are intentionally pursuing an approved second concentration or tailored plan.

### Example interpretation

A student concentrating in **Applied Computing** does:

- the **Applied Computing sector courses**
- plus **12 credits of Applied Computing concentration coursework**
- plus a **second sector sequence** in another area such as Industrial and Manufacturing Systems
- but they do **not** also take that second area's 12-credit concentration coursework unless separately approved

### What the RAG system should not say

The system should **not** tell users that:

- they only need one sector total
- taking a concentration automatically replaces the second sector
- they must complete full 400-level concentration electives for both sectors by default

## Special Advising Notes for the RAG System

### Double concentration note

A student may be able to do **two concentrations**, but this is **not the default interpretation** of the degree.

The system should say:

- this usually requires speaking with an advisor
- this should not be assumed automatically from normal sector requirements

### Tailored concentration note

The system should say:

- tailored concentration plans require advisor guidance and program approval
- the exact course list may vary by student

### Catalog and advising caution

The system should avoid pretending there is certainty when there is not.

It should say things like:

- "Based on the ISAT curriculum structure, you usually need two sectors, and one of those may become your concentration area."
- "For a tailored concentration or two-concentration plan, confirm with an advisor."
- "Course availability, rotation, and prerequisites may affect planning."

## RAG Answering Rules

Use these rules when generating answers.

### Rule 1: Distinguish clearly between core, sector, and concentration

The system must separate:

- lower-level core requirements
- holistic problem-solving requirements
- sector requirements
- concentration requirements

### Rule 2: Do not merge sectors and concentrations into one thing

A sector is **not** the same as a concentration.

Use this interpretation:

- **sector** = the junior-level area sequence, generally 300-level
- **concentration** = the advanced specialization in one area, generally 400-level

### Rule 3: Explain the two-sector model correctly

The system should usually explain that ISAT students complete **two sectors**, and then go deeper in one area through concentration coursework.

### Rule 4: Do not invent sector course numbers if they are missing from the source material

If the system does not have reliable sector-course data for a specific area, it should say that the student needs the official catalog or advisor for the exact junior-level sector sequence.

### Rule 5: Be careful with credit totals

The system should not casually add up concentration electives and assume that is the student's full junior/senior requirement. Sector credits and concentration credits are related but not identical.

### Rule 6: Tailored and double-concentration questions need caution

Whenever the user asks about:

- customizing a concentration
- mixing areas unusually
- doing two concentrations
- swapping required courses

the system should answer carefully and recommend advisor confirmation.

## Safe Example Responses for the RAG System

### Example 1

**Question:** If I want to do Applied Computing, do I only take Applied Computing classes?

**Safe answer:**
No. In ISAT, students generally complete two sector areas. If you choose Applied Computing as your concentration area, you would complete the Applied Computing sector sequence, then the Applied Computing concentration coursework, and also complete a second sector in another area. You do not usually complete the full concentration electives for that second area unless you are doing an approved second concentration or tailored plan.

### Example 2

**Question:** What is the difference between a sector and a concentration?

**Safe answer:**
A sector is the junior-level sequence in one strategic area, usually built from 300-level coursework. A concentration is the deeper specialization in one of those areas, usually using 400-level coursework. ISAT students complete two sectors, then specialize further in one area for their concentration.

### Example 3

**Question:** Can I do two concentrations?

**Safe answer:**
Possibly, but that is not the default assumption for ISAT degree planning. Students interested in doing two concentrations should speak with an advisor to confirm whether their course plan works and whether any formal approvals are needed.
